# Open Source Respect Guidelines

## Why this exists

Open source is built by people who invest time, attention, and responsibility.  
This guideline sets expectations for respectful collaboration between users, contributors, and maintainers.

## Core principles

- Respect people behind the code.
- Treat feedback as collaboration, not entitlement.
- Be clear, factual, and constructive.
- Value maintainers' time and project boundaries.

## For users

- Do not assume free support or immediate response.
- Read existing docs/issues before opening a new issue.
- Report problems with reproducible steps, versions, and logs.
- Ask for help respectfully; avoid demanding language.

## For contributors

- Submit focused changes with clear context.
- Follow project contribution rules and review standards.
- Keep discussion technical and solution-oriented.
- Accept review feedback without personal escalation.

## For maintainers

- Communicate scope and priorities transparently.
- Close or reject requests with clear reasons.
- Keep decisions consistent and documented.
- Protect project health over feature pressure.

## Communication standards

- No harassment, insults, discrimination, or intimidation.
- No personal attacks during technical disagreement.
- No public shaming of unpaid/volunteer maintainers.
- No repeated pressure after a clear maintainer decision.

## Issue and PR etiquette

- One topic per issue/PR.
- Include expected behavior vs actual behavior.
- Mark urgency honestly; do not label everything critical.
- If a change is business-critical for your company, consider sponsoring or implementing it.

## Time, support, and ownership

- Open source maintainers are not default on-call engineers.
- Project roadmaps are not customer backlogs unless explicitly agreed.
- Commercial usage does not automatically include SLA or custom support.

## Enforcement

Project maintainers may moderate discussions, lock threads, reject contributions, or restrict participation when this guideline is violated.

## Final note

Respect is an engineering multiplier.  
Healthy collaboration produces better software than pressure ever will.
